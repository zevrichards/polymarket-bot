POLYMARKET BOT STRATEGIES -- WHAT THEY DO AND WHY THEY'RE LOSING
====================================================================
Last updated: 2026-06-25, after paper-trading all three strategies through
several rounds of bug fixes and a clean, contamination-free test run.

This file is a plain-language reference for what each bot actually does,
since the mechanics are easy to misstate casually. See
BUILD_INTELLIGENCE_REPORT.md for the technical bug/fix history; this file
is about the trading logic itself, not the code.


--------------------------------------------------------------------
BOT 1: DIRECTIONAL ("buy the favorite cheap")
--------------------------------------------------------------------

What it does:
  Polymarket runs short-duration "Bitcoin Up or Down" markets (5 min, 15
  min, and longer "Bitcoin above $X" threshold markets). Each market has
  two outcome tokens -- e.g. "Up" and "Down" -- that the market prices
  between $0 and $1, roughly like a probability. As a market nears its
  resolution time, if one side becomes heavily favored, its price climbs
  toward $1 (the other side's price falls toward $0).

  Bot 1 watches for a market where one side's price is between $0.85 and
  $0.99, with somewhere between 5 and 120 seconds left before the market
  resolves. It buys that favored side, betting it holds and pays out $1.

  This is BETTING WITH the market's own stated consensus, not against it
  -- we only ever buy whichever side the market already thinks is
  winning. The idea is that a $0.85-$0.99 price still leaves a little bit
  of "edge" to capture if the favorite is even more likely to win than
  the price implies.

Why it's losing money:
  At an average entry price of about $0.96, the payout is asymmetric: win
  and you make $0.04, lose and you lose the full $0.96. To break even you
  need to win roughly 96% of the time. In our cleanest test (11 trades
  each), the bots won 54.5% and 63.6% of the time -- meaningfully more
  than half, but nowhere near the ~96% bar this price level demands. One
  wrong call erases the gains from dozens of right ones.

  There is no independent edge built into this strategy at all -- no
  forecast, no model, nothing except trusting the market's own price.
  These are highly liquid, fast-moving markets; there's no particular
  reason to expect the crowd's stated probability to be wrong in our
  favor specifically.

  Note: the real-world "proof" cited when this idea was sourced (wallets
  turning small balances into large ones) was specifically described as
  WEATHER market trades, which had a genuine independent edge (real
  forecast data compared against market price). Nobody ever demonstrated
  this particular BTC mechanical idea working. We tested an unproven
  idea, three separate times, and got a consistent "no edge" result each
  time.


--------------------------------------------------------------------
BOT 2: ORACLE-VERIFIED ("Bot 1 + a sanity check")
--------------------------------------------------------------------

What it does:
  Identical entry rule to Bot 1, but gated behind an independent
  Chainlink BTC/USD price read before trading. Two checks must pass:
    1. The price feed must have updated recently (not stale/disconnected).
    2. BTC's price must not have moved more than a configured threshold
       since our last check (a sudden jump suggests the market is actively
       repricing, and the "favorite" might be about to flip).
  If either check fails, the scan is skipped -- no trade that cycle.

Why it's losing money:
  Same root cause as Bot 1 -- this is a risk *guard* around the same
  entry rule, not a different source of edge. It doesn't predict
  anything; it just occasionally refuses to take the trade Bot 1 would
  have taken, when the oracle looks unreliable in that moment. The
  underlying bet is identical, so the same math applies: you need ~96%
  wins at this price level, and we're not close.


--------------------------------------------------------------------
BOT 3: MARKET MAKER ("capture the spread")
--------------------------------------------------------------------

What it does:
  Instead of betting on a direction, this bot tries to profit from
  providing liquidity. For each outcome token (e.g. the "Up" token), it
  offers to BUY slightly below the current mid-price and SELL slightly
  above it -- e.g. if the mid is $0.50 and the configured spread is
  $0.10, it offers to buy at $0.45 and sell at $0.55. (Important: "mid
  price" here means the midpoint of that outcome token's own $0-$1
  price, NOT Bitcoin's actual dollar price. We never trade real BTC.)

  The bot re-centers ("requotes") this offer periodically as the token's
  real price moves. If real market activity crosses through our offered
  price -- someone's willing to sell at or below our buy price, or buy at
  or above our sell price -- we simulate that fill.

  The profit only comes if BOTH legs eventually fill: buy low, then later
  sell high. The $0.10 gap between the two is the profit margin, but only
  if both sides actually get taken before the market resolves.

How we test this without risking money:
  We never place a real order on Polymarket -- there's no wallet, no
  funds, nothing touching their real order book. Instead, each scan we
  remember the price we said we'd quote last time, then check
  Polymarket's REAL live order book (a genuine, free, read-only check of
  actual market data). If the real market's price has moved enough to
  cross our remembered quote, we treat that as a simulated fill at our
  quoted price, and update a pretend cash/inventory balance accordingly.
  So it's a real-data-driven simulation, not a guess -- we're asking "would
  an order at this exact price have actually executed against the real
  market," using real prices, just without ever placing one. The
  limitation: we only check once every 60 seconds, so a fill that
  happened and reversed in between checks could be missed or
  miscounted.

Why it's losing money (and worse than it first looked):
  The raw "win/loss" count looked roughly neutral (about 50/50), which
  seemed fine for a strategy that isn't supposed to need a directional
  edge. But most of those "resolutions" were quotes that never got
  filled at all (zero inventory the entire time) -- they're free, riskless
  non-events that were inflating the count without representing real
  outcomes. Once filtered down to only the resolutions where the bot
  actually held a real position, the true picture is much worse: it won
  only about 12% of the time it actually had money on the line.

  This is a textbook market-making failure called adverse selection.
  Posting a static buy/sell offer works well when prices bounce around
  randomly -- you buy a dip, sell a bounce, repeat, pocket the spread.
  It works badly when prices are moving steadily in one direction, which
  is exactly what happens as a short-duration market converges toward
  its real outcome. In that situation, you tend to only get the "wrong"
  leg filled: someone sells to you cheap specifically because they know
  (or correctly guess) the price is about to keep falling, and it never
  bounces back up to your sell price. You're left holding inventory
  heading toward a $0 payout. Widening the spread and quoting further
  from resolution helped a little (the real win rate went from
  essentially 0% to about 12%) but didn't fix the underlying problem,
  because the problem isn't pricing -- it's that we have no way to tell,
  at the moment of a fill, whether the person trading against us knows
  something we don't.


--------------------------------------------------------------------
THE COMMON THREAD
--------------------------------------------------------------------

None of the three strategies has an actual INFORMATION EDGE -- something
that estimates the true probability of an outcome independently of the
market's own price.

  - Bots 1/2 take the market's price at face value and bet it's slightly
    underconfident. No independent estimate of the true probability
    exists anywhere in the code.
  - Bot 3 assumes balanced, uninformed order flow, which doesn't exist in
    the final minutes before a fast-resolving market settles.

Contrast with the weather-trading bot referenced early in this project
(github.com/alteregoeth-ai/weatherbot): its edge was a genuine independent
forecast (three weather data sources) compared against the market price,
with confidence intervals calibrated per city from actual resolved
trades. That's the structural piece missing here -- there's no equivalent
independent signal for "what will BTC do in the next 5 minutes" built
into any of these three bots.

Any future strategy for this project should start by answering: what is
the independent signal, and why would it disagree with the market's own
price in our favor? If there's no good answer to that, the strategy is
likely to fail the same way these three did, regardless of execution
quality.
